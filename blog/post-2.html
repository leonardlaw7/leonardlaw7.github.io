Building financial models with Claude

May 13, 2026 · 2 min read

Building financial models is a core part of an analyst's job — but it's also one of the most time-consuming. Earlier this year, I experimented with Claude to see whether AI could accelerate this process, and the results have been genuinely impressive.

The Workflow

The workflow I've developed is straightforward. For a recent China Oil & Gas model, I uploaded three files into a Claude project:

  • a detailed instructions file outlining the rules for data input and formatting
  • a blank Excel financial model template (to standardise with my usual models)
  • the company's annual reports in PDF

With those inputs in place, Claude reads the reports, extracts the relevant financial data year by year, reconciles line items across reporting periods, and populates the model — including a three-year projection driven by an assumptions sheet. What would have taken me (or a junior analyst) the better part of a day was done in 30 minutes.

Quality of Output

The quality of the output has held up well. Claude handles the fiddly parts that eat up analyst time: standardising line items across years, catching when a company restates prior-year figures, and building forecast formulas that are logically tied to the assumptions.

One Limitation Worth Flagging

That said, there is one significant limitation worth flagging: token consumption. I hit a hard limit when I tried to feed in ten years of annual reports all at once. The session timed out after around five hours, which was frustrating given how close I was to a complete model.

The lesson learned is to be strategic about what you upload. If you already have historical data populated in your template, you can save meaningful tokens by simply uploading the most recent year's annual report and asking Claude to update the model incrementally, rather than rebuilding everything from scratch.

Overall

The workflow has been a meaningful time-saver. It won't replace analyst judgment — you still need to sense-check the numbers, understand the business, and make your own calls on projections — but it dramatically compresses the mechanical work of model construction. For anyone in credit research looking to improve their workflow efficiency, it's a low-hanging fruit worth exploring.
