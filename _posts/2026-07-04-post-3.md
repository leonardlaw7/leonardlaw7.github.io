The most useful question about AI at work is not whether you use it. It is how.

A recent MIT Sloan study makes that point well. Researchers watched 244 junior consultants at Boston Consulting Group work on a strategic investment recommendation using a custom GPT-4 tool. They found three distinct ways of working with the AI. Each one changed both the quality of the output and what the person learned along the way.

## The three modes

**Centaur.** The centaur keeps a clear line between human and machine. You decide what you need, then ask the AI narrow, specific questions to get it. The AI handles defined tasks. The judgement stays with you. In the study, centaurs were the smallest group at 14 percent.

**Cyborg.** The cyborg blends with the AI. You work with it through every stage, going back and forth, testing its suggestions, taking some and rejecting others. It is a running conversation rather than a set of discrete tasks. Cyborgs were the largest group at 60 percent.

**Self-automator.** The self-automator hands the task over almost entirely. You feed in the inputs, take the polished output, and move on. The analytical thinking is delegated. Self-automators made up 27 percent, and the authors suspect the real figure across most workplaces is higher.

The names are a good mental model. A centaur has a human head on a horse's body. The parts are separate and each does its own job. A cyborg is fused, human and machine in one.

## What the study found

The results were not what many would guess.

Centaurs produced the most accurate recommendations. They beat both cyborgs and self-automators on getting the answer right. On persuasiveness, centaurs and cyborgs were roughly level, and self-automators trailed.

The learning gap was starker. Centaurs leaned on their own domain expertise and sharpened it further through targeted questions. Several said they worked this way on purpose, to avoid becoming too dependent on the tool. Cyborgs gained little domain expertise, but became much better at solving problems with AI. Self-automators saw no skills gains at all.

Polished is not the same as correct. The self-automators produced clean memos that lacked depth. That gap is the whole point.

## Why this matters for credit

Credit research is a judgement business. The numbers are necessary but they are not the answer. The answer sits in how you read a cash flow story, a covenant package, a management team, or a shifting macro backdrop. This is truest for the hardest names, the stressed and complex credits where the data is ambiguous and the market is split.

That is exactly where over-delegation is dangerous. If you offload the analysis, you get a tidy write-up and you lose the intuition that catches what is wrong. In credit the downside is asymmetric. One missed red flag in an indenture, or one unchallenged cash flow assumption, can cost far more than the time it saved. A polished memo that reaches the wrong conclusion is worse than no memo.

The self-automator trap is seductive precisely because the output looks good. It reads like work. It is not always analysis.

## How I try to work

I build a lot of AI into my workflow. Automated tear sheets, covenant extraction, a research archive that Claude can query. My rule is simple. Automate the mechanical work. Keep the judgement human.

The MIT authors point to task criticality as the deciding factor, and that matches how I think about it. Data extraction, formatting, and first-pass summaries are low risk and repetitive, so they are fine to automate. The credit view itself is high stakes, so it stays with me. On that part I try to stay a centaur. I ask the tool specific questions, I check its work against the primary documents, and I make the call myself.

The goal is not to use less AI. I use more of it every month. The goal is to use it in a way that keeps me sharper, not one that quietly hollows out the expertise that makes the judgement worth anything.

Because in this job, the judgement is the job.
